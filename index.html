Scheller's Superior Human-in-the-Loop (S.H.I.T.)™ Evaluation Framework

A structured methodology for creating objective, repeatable evaluation criteria — and using them to drive iterative improvement until excellence is achieved.

Purpose

Most evaluation is subjective. Two reviewers look at the same presentation and reach different conclusions because they’re using different, unstated criteria. The result? Feedback that’s vague, inconsistent, and hard to act on.

RubricIQ changes that. This tool helps you build structured rubrics with explicit criteria, weighted by importance, with clear definitions of what each performance level looks like — from Missing (0) to Exemplary (4). Every evaluator scores against the same standard. Every score has evidence. Every gap has a clear path to improvement.

The goal isn’t just to grade — it’s to create a feedback loop that drives improvement.

The Test-First Philosophy

Great software engineers write the test before they write the code. They define what “passing” looks like up front, then build until the test passes. This is called test-driven development, and it’s one of the most reliable ways to produce quality work.

The S.H.I.T. Loop applies the same principle to any creative or professional output:

  1. Define Excellence First — Before creating anything, define exactly what a score of 4 (Exemplary) looks like for each criterion. This is your “test.”
  2. Create Your Work — Build, present, write, or deliver your output.
  3. Evaluate Against the Rubric — Score each criterion honestly. The rubric tells you exactly where you stand.
  4. Improve Based on Feedback — For every criterion below Exemplary, the rubric shows you exactly what’s needed to level up.
  5. Iterate Until You Pass — Revise and re-evaluate. The loop continues until you meet your quality threshold (default: 96%).

This is not a one-and-done grading tool. It’s an iterative refinement engine.

Track Progress Over Iterations

Every evaluation is tagged with an iteration number. The first time you score a presentation, that’s Iteration 1. The presenter improves based on your feedback, presents again, and you score Iteration 2. And so on.

The Dashboard tracks this progression automatically:

  • Progress charts — A visual line graph showing how each presenter’s score improves (or doesn’t) across iterations, with the pass threshold marked as a goal line.
  • Trend indicators — At a glance, see who’s improving ↑, declining ↓, stable →, or has passed ✓.
  • Iteration comparison — When viewing any evaluation, see exactly which criteria improved, declined, or stayed the same vs. the previous iteration.
  • Average iterations to pass — How many rounds does it typically take to reach the quality threshold?

This is where the S.H.I.T. Loop comes alive. You’re not just grading — you’re watching improvement happen in real time. The goal is never to assign a final grade. The goal is to close the gap between where you are and where Exemplary lives.

Building Better Criteria

The hardest part of rubric design is writing level definitions that are specific enough to be useful. Saying “Good presentation skills” doesn’t help anyone improve. Saying “Maintains eye contact with audience for 80%+ of delivery, varies vocal tone to emphasize key points, and pauses effectively after important statements” — that’s actionable.

When building your rubric, think about each criterion this way:

  • 4 Exemplary: What does perfection look like? Be specific. This is the target everyone is aiming for.
  • 3 Strong: What’s clearly competent but missing that extra polish? What separates good from great?
  • 2 Adequate: What meets minimum expectations but needs improvement? What’s “acceptable but forgettable”?
  • 1 Weak: What shows an attempt but falls short? What are the common mistakes at this level?
  • 0 Missing: What does absence or complete failure look like?

The more specific your level definitions, the more useful the feedback becomes. A well-designed rubric practically writes the improvement plan for you.

The Scoring System

Each criterion is scored 0–4 and multiplied by its weight (1–5). The composite score is calculated as:

Score = Σ(scorei × weighti) ÷ Σ(4 × weighti) × 100%

Weights (1–5)

Not all criteria matter equally. A weight of 5 means this criterion is five times more important than a weight of 1.

Hard Guardrails

Instant fail conditions. Plagiarism, no-show, safety violations — no score can compensate.

Soft Guardrails

Penalty deductions (1–10%). Serious issues that don’t warrant automatic failure but reduce the final score.

How to Use This Tool

1
Build your rubric (Builder tab) — Define criteria, weights, and level descriptions. Start from the example or create your own. Remember: define Exemplary (4) first — that’s your test case.
2
Score presentations (Scorer tab) — Select your rubric, score each criterion 0–4, and add commentary explaining your reasoning. The system shows you what’s needed to reach Excellent for any criterion below 4.
3
Review results (Dashboard tab) — See all evaluations, compare scores, view presenter averages, and generate printable PDF reports with improvement suggestions.
4
Iterate — Share the report. The presenter improves. Re-evaluate. Repeat until the score passes the threshold. This is the loop.
